That assumes a **0.1c effective expansion front**: not one ship coasting forever, but self-replicating probes/settlements that travel, build, launch the next wave, and keep spreading. That is already extremely aggressive but still physically sane. At that radius, you do **not** “colonize the universe”; you colonize a large chunk of the nearby cosmic web.

The physics is basically:

[
R \approx v_{\text{front}} t
]

So in **1 billion years**:

| Effective front speed |      Radius reached | Diameter of colonized bubble | My judgment                                                 |
| --------------------: | ------------------: | ---------------------------: | ----------------------------------------------------------- |
|                 0.01c |       10 million ly |                20 million ly | conservative interstellar expansion                         |
|                 0.05c |       50 million ly |               100 million ly | very strong but believable                                  |
|              **0.1c** |  **100 million ly** |           **200 million ly** | **my realistic estimate**                                   |
|                  0.2c |      200 million ly |               400 million ly | extreme but not impossible for probes                       |
|                  0.5c |      500 million ly |                 1 billion ly | requires absurd energy/logistics                            |
|                  0.9c | ~900–930 million ly |              ~1.8 billion ly | physics allows tiny probes; not realistic bulk colonization |

Cosmic expansion barely matters over **1 billion years** at these distances. The current Hubble expansion rate is around **70–76 km/s/Mpc**, with the universe about **13.8 billion years old**, so a 1-billion-year expansion campaign is still mostly a local-universe problem, not a horizon problem. ([NASA Science][1])

## Why I would not use 0.9c as the realistic answer

At **0.9c**, the Lorentz factor is about **2.29**. The kinetic energy is:

[
E_k=(\gamma-1)mc^2
]

For **1 kg**, that is about **1.16 × 10¹⁷ joules**, roughly **28 megatons of TNT per kilogram**, before deceleration losses, shielding, inefficiency, launch infrastructure, and crashes into dust grains. That is not impossible by physics, but it is not realistic for repeated civilization-scale colonization. It is plausible only for very small, very expensive, hardened seed probes.

So: **0.9c gets you almost a billion light-years in a billion years**, but the realistic colonization-front estimate is closer to **0.05c–0.2c**, with **0.1c** as the clean central answer.

## What places would be reached at ~0.1c: 100 million ly

A 100-million-light-year radius includes:

**Definitely inside:**

* Milky Way
* Large and Small Magellanic Clouds
* Andromeda / M31
* Triangulum / M33
* the whole Local Group
* M81 Group
* Sculptor Group
* Centaurus A / M83 Group
* Maffei Group
* Canes Venatici groups
* Leo groups
* Virgo Cluster
* Fornax Cluster
* Eridanus Group
* Dorado Group
* Antlia Cluster
* most of the Local / Virgo Supercluster region

The **Virgo Cluster**, about **65 million light-years** away and containing about **2,000 galaxies**, is well inside the realistic 0.1c bubble. ([Imagine the Universe][2])

**Not reached at 0.1c, but reached by faster fronts:**

* **Great Attractor / Norma region**: ~150–250 million ly
* **Perseus–Pisces Supercluster**: ~250 million ly
* **Coma Cluster**: ~320 million ly; over 1,000 identified galaxies ([Wikipedia][3])
* **Shapley Supercluster**: ~650 million ly, one of the most massive local-universe concentrations ([HEASARC][4])
* **Vela / Vela-Banzi Supercluster**: ~800–870 million ly, reachable only in the ~0.9c case within 1 billion years ([Space][5])

## Number of galaxies and stars

For the **realistic 0.1c / 100 million ly radius case**:

* **Galaxies:** roughly **10,000–100,000 galaxies**
* **Stars:** roughly **10¹⁴–10¹⁵ stars**
* **Useful long-lived stars:** roughly **10¹⁴-ish**, dominated by K and M dwarfs

That includes many dwarf galaxies, not just big Milky-Way-sized spirals.

For the **extreme 0.9c / ~900 million ly radius case**:

* **Galaxies:** roughly **1–15 million galaxies**
* **Stars:** roughly **10¹⁷ stars**
* **Long-lived useful stars:** tens of quadrillions to ~100 quadrillion scale

The galaxy count has a wide range because dwarf galaxies dominate the count and are hard to census. Hubble-based estimates raised the possible observable-universe galaxy count by about a factor of ten versus older estimates, up to the trillion-scale, especially due to faint galaxies. ([NASA Science][6])

## Which stars make sense

For a billion-year-plus colonization project, the best targets are not O, B, or A stars. They burn too fast or are too hostile.

Best practical targets:

| Star type          |                Makes sense? | Why                                                 |
| ------------------ | --------------------------: | --------------------------------------------------- |
| **K dwarfs**       |                    **Best** | Long-lived, stable, less violent than many M dwarfs |
| **quiet M dwarfs** | **Good for infrastructure** | Extremely long-lived, abundant, but flare/UV issues |
| **G dwarfs**       |              Good but rarer | Sun-like; ~10-billion-year lifetime                 |
| **F dwarfs**       |                    Marginal | Shorter lifetimes, higher radiation                 |
| **A/B/O stars**    |         No, except industry | Too short-lived and harsh                           |

NASA notes that the Sun lasts about **10 billion years**, K dwarfs can last **15–45 billion years**, red dwarfs make up about **73%** of the Milky Way’s stars, K dwarfs about **13%**, and Sun-like stars about **6%**. ([NASA Science][7])

So the colonization strategy is: **prioritize K dwarfs, use quiet older M dwarfs heavily, include G dwarfs, ignore massive blue stars except as temporary industrial sites.**

## Final answer

**Realistic 1-billion-year colonization radius: ~100 million light-years.**

That means **the Local Group, Virgo Cluster, Fornax Cluster, and much of the Local/Virgo Supercluster** are colonized. Call it **tens of thousands of galaxies** and **hundreds of trillions to a quadrillion stars**, with the most useful long-term targets being **K dwarfs and quiet M dwarfs**.

**Absolute physics-max with 0.9c seed probes:** nearly **1 billion light-years**, reaching things like **Coma, Shapley, Perseus–Pisces, and Vela-Banzi**, with **millions of galaxies** and around **10¹⁷ stars**. But that is not the realistic civilization-scale answer; it is the “tiny relativistic probe swarm with insane energy budget” answer.
